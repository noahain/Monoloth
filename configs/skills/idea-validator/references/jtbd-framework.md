# Jobs-to-be-Done Framework

Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) is a theory of innovation originating from Clayton Christensen's work and refined by Anthony Ulwick (Outcome-Driven Innovation). The core insight: customers do not buy products — they hire solutions to make progress in specific circumstances. Understanding the job unlocks why people switch between solutions and what a new entrant must deliver to win adoption.

## Job Statement Format

A well-formed job statement follows this structure:

> When I [situation/context], I want to [motivation/action], so I can [expected outcome/desired progress].

Examples:

- When I am preparing for a board meeting, I want to quickly assemble financial reports, so I can present a confident narrative about company performance.
- When I move to a new city, I want to find a trustworthy dentist, so I can maintain my family's health without research anxiety.
- When I am onboarding a new hire, I want to get them productive within their first week, so I can justify the headcount to leadership.

The job is stable over time. The solutions people hire change; the underlying job does not. People have been "hiring" solutions to "stay informed about current events" for centuries — newspapers, radio, TV, Twitter, newsletters.

## Three Job Types

### Functional Job

What the customer is trying to accomplish. The practical, task-oriented goal.

- Measurable and observable
- Can be decomposed into steps (job map)
- Examples: file taxes, schedule meetings, track expenses, deploy code

### Emotional Job

How the customer wants to feel during and after completing the job.

- Personal emotional states: feel confident, reduce anxiety, feel in control
- Often the real driver behind functional job selection
- Examples: feel secure about retirement savings, feel confident presenting data, feel relieved that compliance is handled

### Social Job

How the customer wants to be perceived by others.

- External perception and status
- Professional identity and reputation
- Examples: be seen as innovative, appear organized, demonstrate technical competence, signal good taste

A complete JTBD analysis addresses all three types. Solutions that nail the functional job but ignore emotional and social dimensions lose to competitors that address the full job.

## Hiring and Firing Framework

Customers "hire" solutions to do a job and "fire" them when something better comes along or when the solution fails to deliver.

### Hiring Criteria

When a customer adopts a new solution, they are hiring it for:

- The functional outcome it delivers
- The emotional relief or satisfaction it provides
- The social signal it sends

Questions to assess:

- What solution does the customer currently hire for this job?
- How well does the current solution perform? (satisfaction level)
- What are the workarounds the customer uses to compensate for current solution gaps?
- What would the customer hire if their current solution disappeared tomorrow?

### Firing Criteria

When a customer abandons a solution:

- What triggered the switch? (specific event or accumulation)
- What was the last straw?
- What did they switch to?
- What do they miss about the fired solution?

Workarounds are gold. When customers build spreadsheets, manual processes, or duct-tape solutions around a product's limitations, those workarounds reveal unmet job dimensions.

## Four Forces of Progress

Four forces determine whether a customer will switch from their current solution to a new one. Two forces drive switching forward; two forces resist it.

### Forces Driving Change

**Push of the Current Situation**
Dissatisfaction with the status quo. Pain, frustration, inefficiency, cost.

- "This takes too long"
- "I keep making mistakes with this process"
- "We're paying too much for what we get"

Without push, there is no motivation to change. The stronger the push, the more willing the customer is to tolerate friction in the switch.

**Pull of the New Solution**
Attraction to the imagined better future. The promise of the new solution.

- "This would save us 10 hours a week"
- "This looks so much easier"
- "Competitors are using this and getting results"

Pull without push produces tire-kickers — people who admire the new solution but never actually switch.

### Forces Resisting Change

**Anxiety of the New Solution**
Fear and uncertainty about whether the new solution will work.

- "What if it breaks our existing workflow?"
- "What if we lose data during migration?"
- "What if the team won't adopt it?"
- "What if this startup goes out of business?"

Anxiety is reduced by: free trials, migration support, case studies, reversibility, gradual rollout options.

**Habit of the Current Solution**
Comfort and familiarity with existing workflows, even if they are suboptimal.

- "We've always done it this way"
- "Everyone knows how to use the current tool"
- "It's not great but it's good enough"
- Sunk cost ("We already invested in this")

Habit is the most underestimated force. Solutions must not only be better — they must be sufficiently better to overcome the energy cost of change.

### Switching Calculus

For a customer to switch:

> (Push + Pull) > (Anxiety + Habit)

If Push + Pull does not exceed Anxiety + Habit, the customer stays with the current solution regardless of how objectively superior the new one is.

### Implications for Idea Validation

- **High push, low pull:** The problem is real but your solution is not compelling enough. Improve the solution or its communication.
- **High pull, low push:** Your solution looks great but the status quo is tolerable. Target customers with the most acute version of the problem.
- **High anxiety:** Reduce risk for the customer. Offer free trials, migration guarantees, pilot programs.
- **High habit:** Make switching gradual. Integrate with existing workflows rather than replacing them entirely.

## Assessing Solution-Job Fit

A solution addresses the core job when:

1. **Functional coverage:** The solution performs the primary functional job at least as well as the current best alternative, plus addresses at least one underserved dimension.
2. **Emotional alignment:** Using the solution produces the desired emotional state (confidence, relief, control) rather than introducing new anxieties.
3. **Social reinforcement:** The solution does not undermine the customer's social positioning (e.g., a "cheap" solution hired by someone who wants to signal premium taste).
4. **Progress continuity:** The solution enables the customer to make progress without requiring them to change their identity or fundamental workflow.

Rate each dimension:

- **Strong fit:** Solution clearly outperforms current alternatives on this dimension
- **Adequate fit:** Solution matches current alternatives
- **Weak fit:** Solution underperforms or introduces new friction
- **Missing:** Solution does not address this dimension at all

## Common JTBD Mistakes

**Confusing the job with the solution.** "I need a faster horse" is a solution statement. The job is "travel from A to B quickly and comfortably." Always dig past the solution to find the underlying job.

**Defining the job too broadly.** "Manage my business" is not actionable. "Generate accurate financial reports for monthly board meetings within 2 hours" is.

**Defining the job too narrowly.** "Click the export button" is a task within a job, not the job itself. The job includes the context and desired outcome.

**Ignoring context.** The same person has different jobs in different situations. A CEO's job when preparing for a board meeting is different from their job when reviewing a quarterly plan. Context determines which solution gets hired.

**Assuming the job is rational.** Emotional and social jobs frequently override functional superiority. A functionally inferior solution wins when it better serves the emotional or social job.
