# SWOT and PESTLE Analysis Frameworks

## SWOT Analysis

SWOT categorizes factors affecting an idea along two axes: internal vs external, and helpful vs harmful.

```
              Helpful          Harmful
           ┌──────────────┬──────────────┐
Internal   │  Strengths   │  Weaknesses  │
           ├──────────────┼──────────────┤
External   │ Opportunities│   Threats    │
           └──────────────┴──────────────┘
```

### Internal Factors (Strengths & Weaknesses)

Internal factors are within the team's control. They describe what the venture brings to the table today and where it falls short.

**Strengths** — internal capabilities that provide advantage:

- Team expertise and domain knowledge
- Proprietary technology or data
- Existing customer relationships or distribution
- Capital reserves or funding secured
- Brand recognition or reputation
- Operational efficiency or cost advantages
- Intellectual property (patents, trade secrets)

**Weaknesses** — internal limitations that create disadvantage:

- Skill gaps in the team
- Limited capital or runway
- No existing distribution channel
- Technical debt or immature architecture
- Lack of domain expertise
- Small team with no redundancy
- No brand recognition

### External Factors (Opportunities & Threats)

External factors are outside the team's control. They describe market conditions, industry dynamics, and environmental forces.

**Opportunities** — external conditions that could be exploited:

- Growing market demand
- Regulatory changes creating new needs
- Technology shifts enabling new approaches
- Competitor weaknesses or market exits
- Changing customer behavior or preferences
- New distribution channels emerging
- Partnership possibilities

**Threats** — external conditions that could cause harm:

- New or strengthening competitors
- Regulatory restrictions or compliance costs
- Economic downturn affecting target market
- Technology disruption making the approach obsolete
- Customer behavior shifting away from the model
- Supply chain or vendor risks
- Talent market competition

### Common SWOT Mistakes

**Listing features as strengths.** "Our product has real-time notifications" is a feature, not a strength. A strength is the underlying capability: "Team has 10 years of experience building real-time systems at scale."

**Confusing internal and external.** "Market is growing" is an opportunity (external), not a strength (internal). "We have strong partnerships" is a strength (internal), not an opportunity (external). The test: does the team control it? Internal. Is it imposed by the environment? External.

**Vague entries.** "Good team" is not useful. "CTO has shipped 3 products in this vertical and has relationships with 50+ potential beta customers" is.

**Ignoring weaknesses.** Every venture has weaknesses. An empty weaknesses quadrant indicates incomplete analysis, not a strong position.

**Confusing aspirations with current state.** "We will have network effects" is aspirational. If network effects do not exist today, they are not a current strength.

### SWOT-to-Strategy Matrix

Use the completed SWOT to derive strategic priorities:

**SO Strategies (Strengths × Opportunities)**
Use internal strengths to capture external opportunities. These are the highest-priority strategies — they leverage what you have to seize what is available.

- Example: Team's AI expertise (S) + growing demand for AI compliance tools (O) = Build AI-first compliance automation

**WO Strategies (Weaknesses × Opportunities)**
Address internal weaknesses to unlock external opportunities. These require investment or partnership.

- Example: No sales team (W) + enterprise buyers seeking solutions (O) = Partner with a channel reseller or hire first AE

**ST Strategies (Strengths × Threats)**
Use internal strengths to defend against external threats. These are defensive moves.

- Example: Strong brand in niche (S) + large competitor entering market (T) = Double down on vertical specialization and customer intimacy

**WT Strategies (Weaknesses × Threats)**
Minimize weaknesses and avoid threats. These are survival strategies — if this quadrant dominates, the idea may not be viable.

- Example: Limited runway (W) + economic downturn (T) = Reduce burn, focus on revenue-generating features only, defer growth spending

## PESTLE Analysis

PESTLE scans the macro environment across six categories. It feeds directly into the external quadrants of SWOT (Opportunities and Threats) and into the Timing & Environment dimension of validation scoring.

### Political

Government actions, policies, and political dynamics that affect the business environment.

Sub-factors:

- Government stability and policy direction
- Tax policy and incentives (R&D credits, startup tax breaks)
- Trade restrictions, tariffs, sanctions
- Government spending priorities
- Political attitudes toward the industry (supportive, neutral, hostile)
- International relations affecting cross-border operations
- Lobbying landscape and industry associations

Questions to assess:

- Does the business operate in a politically sensitive area?
- Are there government programs that support or hinder this type of venture?
- Could a change in government alter the regulatory landscape?

### Economic

Market conditions, financial environment, and economic forces.

Sub-factors:

- GDP growth and economic cycle position
- Interest rates and cost of capital
- Venture capital and funding availability
- Consumer/enterprise spending trends
- Inflation and pricing pressure
- Exchange rates (for international operations)
- Employment rates and labor costs
- Industry-specific economic indicators

Questions to assess:

- Is the target customer's budget expanding or contracting?
- How does the economic cycle affect willingness to adopt new solutions?
- Is funding available for this stage and type of venture?

### Social

Demographic trends, cultural shifts, and human behavior patterns.

Sub-factors:

- Demographic shifts (aging, urbanization, remote work)
- Cultural attitudes toward the problem/solution domain
- Consumer behavior trends (privacy awareness, sustainability preference)
- Workforce trends (gig economy, automation anxiety, skill gaps)
- Health and lifestyle trends
- Education levels and digital literacy
- Social media influence and information consumption patterns

Questions to assess:

- Is there a cultural or behavioral trend accelerating demand?
- Does the target demographic prefer this type of solution?
- Are there social resistance factors (privacy concerns, distrust)?

### Technological

Technology landscape, maturity, and trajectory.

Sub-factors:

- Technology maturity for key components (is the required tech production-ready?)
- Infrastructure availability (cloud, APIs, mobile penetration)
- Pace of technological change (is the tech stable or rapidly evolving?)
- Automation and AI adoption in the target industry
- Cybersecurity landscape
- Open-source ecosystem availability
- Platform dependencies and risks (API changes, deplatforming)

Questions to assess:

- Is the required technology mature enough to build on reliably?
- Could a technology shift make this approach obsolete within 3 years?
- Are there emerging technologies that could enhance the solution?

### Legal

Laws, regulations, compliance requirements, and legal risks.

Sub-factors:

- Data privacy regulations (GDPR, CCPA, sector-specific)
- Industry-specific regulations (HIPAA, PCI-DSS, SOX)
- Intellectual property protection (patent landscape, trade secrets)
- Employment law and contractor classification
- Consumer protection laws
- Liability and insurance requirements
- Licensing requirements
- Antitrust and competition law

Questions to assess:

- What compliance requirements must be met before launch?
- Is the IP protectable?
- Are there legal risks specific to this business model?
- Could upcoming legislation create opportunities or threats?

### Environmental

Sustainability, resource constraints, and ecological factors.

Sub-factors:

- Environmental regulations and carbon reporting requirements
- Sustainability expectations from customers and investors
- Resource availability and supply chain sustainability
- Energy costs and computational carbon footprint
- ESG requirements from institutional investors
- Circular economy pressures
- Climate risk to physical operations or supply chains

Questions to assess:

- Does the business have a significant environmental footprint?
- Do customers or investors expect sustainability commitments?
- Are there environmental regulations that affect operations?

## PESTLE Prioritization

Not all PESTLE factors are equally relevant. Prioritize by:

**Impact** — How significantly would this factor affect the business?

- High: could make or break the venture
- Medium: meaningful effect on operations, costs, or growth
- Low: marginal effect, does not change core viability

**Likelihood** — How probable is this factor's influence?

- High: already occurring or near-certain
- Medium: plausible within the planning horizon (1-3 years)
- Low: possible but unlikely

**Priority matrix:**

|                       | High Impact         | Medium Impact    | Low Impact |
| --------------------- | ------------------- | ---------------- | ---------- |
| **High Likelihood**   | Address immediately | Monitor and plan | Note       |
| **Medium Likelihood** | Develop contingency | Monitor          | Ignore     |
| **Low Likelihood**    | Watch for signals   | Ignore           | Ignore     |

Focus analysis on the high-impact, high-likelihood quadrant. Do not waste space on low-impact, low-likelihood factors.

## PESTLE → SWOT Connection

PESTLE outputs feed directly into SWOT external quadrants:

- Favorable PESTLE factors → **Opportunities** (e.g., new regulation creating demand, technology maturation enabling new approach)
- Unfavorable PESTLE factors → **Threats** (e.g., regulatory restriction, economic downturn, technology disruption)

This ensures SWOT external factors are grounded in systematic macro analysis rather than ad-hoc speculation.

## When PESTLE Matters Most

PESTLE is critical when:

- The business operates in a heavily regulated industry (healthcare, finance, education, energy)
- The business operates internationally or plans to expand across borders
- The business model depends on regulatory conditions (cannabis, crypto, gig economy)
- The idea is timing-dependent (infrastructure readiness, cultural adoption curves)
- The business has significant environmental impact or ESG exposure

PESTLE is less critical (brief scan sufficient) when:

- The business is a pure software tool in an unregulated space
- Operations are domestic and single-market
- The macro environment is stable and favorable
- The business model is well-established in the market
