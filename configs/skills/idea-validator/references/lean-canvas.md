# Lean Canvas Framework

The Lean Canvas is a one-page business model adapted from Alex Osterwalder's Business Model Canvas by Ash Maurya. It replaces partner-focused boxes with problem-focused ones, making it suited for early-stage idea validation where the primary risk is building something nobody wants.

## The 9 Boxes

### 1. Problem

The top 3 problems your target customer faces related to the domain you are addressing.

**What belongs:** Specific, observable pain points. Quantifiable where possible (costs time, money, reputation). Problems the customer actively recognizes or can be shown to recognize.

**What does not belong:** Your solution disguised as a problem. Internal technical challenges. Problems only you care about.

**Common mistakes:**

- Stating the problem too broadly ("communication is hard")
- Listing problems the customer does not actually have
- Confusing symptoms with root causes

**Assessment criteria:**

- Are the problems specific enough to test?
- Does the customer acknowledge these problems?
- Is there existing evidence of the problem (forums, complaints, workarounds)?

**Red flags:** Cannot articulate who has the problem. Problem requires educating the market to exist.

### 2. Customer Segments

The target market and early adopter profile.

**What belongs:** Specific demographic, psychographic, or firmographic description. The early adopter subset — who will use this first and forgive imperfection?

**What does not belong:** "Everyone." Multiple unrelated segments without prioritization.

**Common mistakes:**

- Defining the segment too broadly ("small businesses")
- No distinction between mainstream market and early adopters
- Targeting a segment you cannot reach

**Assessment criteria:**

- Can you identify and reach these people?
- Is the early adopter profile specific enough to find 10 of them this week?
- Is the segment large enough to build a business but small enough to dominate initially?

**Red flags:** "Everyone needs this." No early adopter hypothesis. Segment requires the product to already exist at scale.

### 3. Unique Value Proposition

A single, clear, compelling message that states why your solution is different and worth paying attention to.

**What belongs:** A specific promise tied to the customer's problem. What makes you different from the status quo, not just from competitors.

**What does not belong:** Feature lists. Jargon. Generic quality claims ("best-in-class").

**Common mistakes:**

- Describing features instead of outcomes
- Not differentiating from existing alternatives
- Being too abstract to be testable

**Assessment criteria:**

- Does it address the #1 problem directly?
- Would a customer understand it in under 8 seconds?
- Is it falsifiable — could you prove it wrong?

**Red flags:** UVP describes the technology rather than the customer benefit. UVP is indistinguishable from competitors' messaging.

### 4. Solution

The top 3 features that address the top 3 problems.

**What belongs:** Minimum viable features that directly solve the stated problems. Each feature maps to a specific problem.

**What does not belong:** Feature wishlists. Technical architecture details. Features that address problems not listed in box 1.

**Common mistakes:**

- Solution does not map to stated problems
- Over-scoping the MVP
- Describing the solution before validating the problem

**Assessment criteria:**

- Does each feature map to a stated problem?
- Is this the simplest possible solution that tests the hypothesis?
- Can this be built and shipped to early adopters within weeks, not months?

**Red flags:** Solution is a technology looking for a problem. Feature list exceeds what a small team can build in 6 weeks.

### 5. Channels

How you reach and deliver value to customers.

**What belongs:** Specific acquisition channels (content marketing, cold outreach, partnerships, app stores). Distribution channels (web, mobile, API, physical). Support channels.

**What does not belong:** Aspirational channels you have no path to ("viral growth"). Channels that do not reach your stated customer segment.

**Common mistakes:**

- Relying on channels that require scale to work (viral, network effects) before you have scale
- No connection between channel and customer segment
- Ignoring the cost of the channel

**Assessment criteria:**

- Do these channels reach the early adopters identified in box 2?
- Is there at least one channel you can start using today?
- What is the cost per acquired customer through each channel?

**Red flags:** Only channel is "word of mouth" or "going viral." No channel the team has experience or connections in.

### 6. Revenue Streams

How the business makes money.

**What belongs:** Pricing model (subscription, transaction fee, freemium, one-time). Price point or range. Who pays (user, advertiser, enterprise buyer). Revenue timing.

**What does not belong:** "We'll figure out monetization later." Revenue models that contradict the UVP.

**Common mistakes:**

- No pricing hypothesis
- Pricing disconnected from value delivered
- Relying on advertising revenue at small scale

**Assessment criteria:**

- Is the price justified by the value (does it save or earn the customer more than it costs)?
- Is the pricing model standard for this market or does it require behavior change?
- Can you test willingness to pay before building?

**Red flags:** No revenue model. "Free now, monetize later" without a specific plan. Price requires the customer to change purchasing behavior.

### 7. Cost Structure

Fixed and variable costs to operate the business.

**What belongs:** Customer acquisition costs, hosting/infrastructure, team salaries, tools and services, cost of goods sold.

**What does not belong:** Aspirational future costs at scale. Costs for features not in the MVP.

**Assessment criteria:**

- Are costs realistic for the current stage?
- Is the unit economics favorable (revenue per customer > cost per customer)?
- What is the burn rate and runway?

**Red flags:** Costs exceed projected revenue at any achievable scale. High fixed costs with unvalidated demand.

### 8. Key Metrics

The numbers that tell you whether the business is working.

**What belongs:** Activation rate, retention rate, revenue per user, customer acquisition cost, referral rate. Stage-appropriate metrics (pre-product/market fit: engagement and retention; post-fit: growth and revenue).

**What does not belong:** Vanity metrics (page views, downloads, registered users without activation).

**Assessment criteria:**

- Do these metrics connect to the business model?
- Can you measure them with current tools?
- Do they indicate whether the value proposition is working?

**Red flags:** Only tracking vanity metrics. No retention or engagement metrics. Metrics that cannot be measured at current scale.

### 9. Unfair Advantage

Something that cannot be easily copied or bought by competitors.

**What belongs:** Network effects, proprietary data, domain expertise, existing audience, regulatory advantage, switching costs, personal authority, embedded workflows.

**What does not belong:** Features (can be copied), first-mover advantage (rarely durable), passion (not a moat), working harder.

**Common mistakes:**

- Listing features or technology as the advantage
- Claiming first-mover advantage
- Leaving this box blank (honest but concerning)

**Assessment criteria:**

- Would this advantage still hold if a well-funded competitor entered?
- Does the advantage compound over time?
- Is the advantage real today or aspirational?

**Red flags:** No unfair advantage identified. Advantage is "our team" without specific domain expertise. Advantage requires scale the business does not yet have.

## Cross-Box Coherence

The canvas is a system, not a collection of independent boxes. Validate these relationships:

- **Problem → Solution:** Each solution feature addresses a stated problem
- **Problem → Customer Segments:** The stated customer actually experiences these problems
- **Customer Segments → Channels:** The channels can reach the stated segments
- **UVP → Problem:** The value proposition addresses the primary problem
- **Revenue Streams → Customer Segments:** The customer can and will pay the stated price
- **Solution → Cost Structure:** The costs are consistent with building the stated solution
- **Key Metrics → UVP:** The metrics measure whether the value proposition is landing

Incoherence between boxes is a stronger signal than weakness in any individual box.

## Lean Canvas vs Business Model Canvas

| Aspect      | Lean Canvas                                 | Business Model Canvas                             |
| ----------- | ------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Focus       | Problem/solution fit                        | Business model architecture                       |
| Best for    | Early-stage startups, new product ideas     | Established businesses, business model innovation |
| Replaces    | Key Partners, Key Activities, Key Resources | Problem, Solution, Unfair Advantage, Key Metrics  |
| Orientation | Risk and hypothesis testing                 | Value chain and partner ecosystem                 |

Use Lean Canvas when validating whether the idea should exist. Use Business Model Canvas when designing how an established idea operates.

## Prioritization

Ash Maurya's recommended order for filling the canvas:

1. Start with Problem and Customer Segments (who has the problem?)
2. Then Unique Value Proposition (why will they care?)
3. Then Solution (what is the minimum viable approach?)
4. Then Channels and Revenue Streams (how do you reach and charge them?)
5. Then Cost Structure and Key Metrics (what does it cost and how do you measure?)
6. Unfair Advantage last (this often emerges over time)

The canvas is a living document. Expect it to change as assumptions are tested.
